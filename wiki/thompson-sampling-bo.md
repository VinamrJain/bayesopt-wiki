# Thompson Sampling (Bayesian Optimization)

Thompson sampling (TS) is a **randomized acquisition strategy**: rather than
constructing a deterministic score for each candidate and maximizing it, TS
samples a function from the GP posterior and queries its maximizer. Its
explore–exploit balance emerges automatically from posterior uncertainty — no
explicit tradeoff knob like UCB's $\beta_t$ is needed. TS is the natural
*randomized counterpart of optimism*: where [[gp-ucb]] pins a deterministic
upper quantile of the posterior, TS draws a random function whose value at any
$x$ is a draw from the same marginal — the same uncertainty that UCB inflates
deterministically, TS explores stochastically. The posterior over the
argmax — the distribution $P_n(x = x^*)$ that TS implicitly samples from — is
also the seed of the entropy-search family: [[max-value-entropy-search]] and
[[entropy-search]] reason explicitly about $p_\star(x\mid\mathcal{D}_n)$
rather than sampling from it. Symbols follow [[notation]].

## The rule

At step $n+1$, draw one function $g$ from the posterior GP:

$$
g \sim \mathrm{GP}\!\left(\mu_n,\,\Sigma_n\right),
$$

where $\mu_n(\cdot)$ and $\Sigma_n(\cdot,\cdot)$ are the posterior mean and
covariance after observing $\mathcal{D}_n$. Then set

$$
x_{n+1} \in \operatorname*{arg\,max}_{x \in A} g(x).
$$

## Equivalence: argmax of a sample = sample from the argmax distribution

The two phrasings of TS are:

1. **Draw a function**, then maximize it.
2. **Draw a point** from $p_\star(x\mid\mathcal{D}_n)$, the posterior
   probability that $x$ is the global maximizer of $f$.

They are equivalent by construction. The posterior over functions induces a
distribution over $x^* = \operatorname*{arg\,max}_x f(x)$. Drawing $g\sim
\mathrm{GP}(\mu_n,\Sigma_n)$ and taking $\operatorname*{arg\,max}_x g(x)$
produces a sample from exactly that induced distribution $p_\star(x\mid
\mathcal{D}_n)$. Shahriari et al. (`shahriari2016`, §IV-C) express this
directly as the randomized acquisition function

$$
x_{n+1} \sim p_\star\!\left(x\mid\mathcal{D}_n\right),
$$

making the probability-matching interpretation explicit.

## Why it balances exploration and exploitation

No parameter governs the explore–exploit tradeoff: the posterior does it
automatically.

- **Exploitation.** In regions where $\mu_n(x)$ is high and $\sigma_n(x)$ is
  small, every draw $g$ from the posterior is close to $\mu_n$, so
  $\operatorname*{arg\,max} g$ concentrates there.
- **Exploration.** In regions with high $\sigma_n(x)$, the draw $g$ can
  occasionally spike far above $\mu_n$, pushing the argmax into unexplored
  territory. Such excursions happen with probability proportional to posterior
  uncertainty, so under-explored but potentially promising regions are visited
  without a hand-tuned weight.

This contrasts with [[gp-ucb]], whose acquisition $\mu_n(x)+\beta_t\sigma_n(x)$
requires choosing $\beta_t$: too small and it exploits greedily; too large and
it explores wastefully. TS achieves the same qualitative balance via
randomization, with no free parameter beyond the GP hyperparameters.

`shahriari2016` (§IV-A) highlights four practical virtues of this
parameter-free character: no free parameters beyond the prior; natural
exploration–exploitation trade-off from posterior beliefs; easy implementation
given posterior sampling; and particular suitability for batch and
delayed-feedback settings, where multiple candidates $x_{n+1}^{(1)},\ldots$ can
be generated by drawing independent function samples from the same posterior
(`shahriari2016`, §IV-A).

## Practical computation: the sample-path problem

Drawing an exact sample from a GP posterior requires a function $g: A\to
\mathbb{R}$ that can be queried at *any* point — a draw from an
infinite-dimensional object. In the finite discrete case (a fixed candidate set
$\{x^{(1)},\ldots,x^{(M)}\}$) this is straightforward: sample a joint
$M$-dimensional Gaussian and take the argmax. For a continuous domain this is
the central computational challenge.

> **Remark.** `shahriari2016` (§IV-C) states directly: *"it is not clear how
> to fix an exact sample from the GP."* The commonly written procedure "draw
> $g\sim\mathrm{GP}(\mu_n,\Sigma_n)$ and maximize" is therefore an
> approximation prescription, not an exact algorithm, whenever $A$ is
> continuous. The acquisition function formula (eq. 46 in `shahriari2016`)
> annotates the draw with $\stackrel{\text{s.s.}}{\sim}$ to flag this:
> approximate simulation, not exact sampling.

**Standard routes to approximate sample paths (`shahriari2016`, §IV-C):**

- **Spectral (random-feature) approximation.** Bochner's theorem represents a
  stationary kernel as the expected value of a Fourier basis over its spectral
  density. Drawing $m$ frequencies from that spectral density and constructing
  a Bayesian linear model in the resulting random-feature basis yields a finite
  parametric approximation to the GP whose sample paths *can* be queried at
  arbitrary points. The resulting approximate TS sample is then maximized by
  standard continuous optimization. This extends TS to continuous domains and
  is the standard workhorse (`shahriari2016`, §IV-C, citing Hernández-Lobato
  et al. 2014).
- **Finite candidate sets.** Discretize $A$ to a finite grid or random set
  $\{x^{(j)}\}$, draw a joint Gaussian, and take the discrete argmax. Coarse
  enough for cheap inner optimization; accuracy degrades with $d$.

`shahriari2016` (§IV-C) notes that TS empirically performs well but
*deteriorates in high-dimensional problems*, likely because aggressive
exploration becomes less targeted as $d$ grows.

## Connections to other acquisitions

**TS and UCB: randomized vs. deterministic optimism.** UCB evaluates
$\mu_n(x)+\beta_t\sigma_n(x)$ — the $\beta_t$-quantile of the posterior
marginal at $x$ — and maximizes it deterministically. TS evaluates a single
draw $g(x)$ from the same marginal and maximizes it. Both are "optimistic" in
the sense of giving uncertain regions a chance; UCB commits to a fixed quantile
level, TS randomizes across all quantile levels. The two become equivalent in
distribution when $\beta_t$ is chosen to match the quantile of a standard
normal draw, but in practice they produce different sample paths.

**TS and entropy search: sampling vs. reasoning about $p_\star$.**
`shahriari2016` (§IV-C) frames TS and entropy search (ES) together as
*information-based* policies, both centered on the posterior over the optimizer
$p_\star(x\mid\mathcal{D}_n)$. The difference is how each uses it:
TS **samples** from $p_\star$ (one draw, one query); ES **reduces the entropy**
of $p_\star$ by choosing the query point that yields maximum expected
information gain about $x^*$ (see [[max-value-entropy-search]] for the
$f^*$-targeting variant). TS is thus the computationally cheapest member of
this family — no entropy computation, no mutual-information approximation — at
the cost of variance: a single random draw may occasionally make a poor choice.

## Origin and crosswalk

Thompson (1933) introduced probability matching in the Bernoulli bandit;
`shahriari2016` traces the history (§VIII-A) and provides both the bandit
formulation (§III-A) and the GP extension (§IV-C). `frazier2018` does not cover
TS directly (its three acquisition functions are EI, KG, and ES/PES), so
`shahriari2016` is the primary source for this note.
